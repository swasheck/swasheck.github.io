Covid-19: Our First Week

Mar 20, 2020 Categories: personal Tags: covid-19

Last week I finished what would end up being my last workout for who-knows-how-long. The gym was eerily quiet. It was just me and a few regulars. Covid-19 had already made inroads into Douglas County but, for some reason, I still went and worked out. Friday is “leg day” - my favorite day. After an ACL replacement and a subsequent MCL sprain, I've come to love leg day. It's been a journey to get back to the days when I was actually athletic and played baseball and swam competitively (but that was High School). At 42.5 years old, doing 48” box jumps bring me a sense of personal satisfaction that I never thought I'd have when I was 17. So I got one last workout in.

In retrospect, maybe it wasn't worth it. I'd hate to have been a carrier. I'm always very diligent about overusing the Virex that they provide on the yoga mats and other equipment that I use but there's just so much uncertainty right now.

Since that time, I've hypocritically (or so I feel) been beating the drum for quarantine (which we've been living) or “social distancing” or whatever it is that we're doing. I've felt so many feelings. Despair. Sadness. Anger. I'm writing this as therapy for myself, but also in the hopes that we don't feel alone in the pit.

I've watched as our family of 5 have taken drastic measures to distance ourselves from the people we love the most. We've been the first of our closest friends to say, “no” to spending time together - time that we so desperately crave with these folks. That's already caused some damage to those friendships. Hurt feelings can wreck a friendship - especially when the means of repairing them don't look the same as they did even a week ago.

I've watched as our family of 5 have taken drastic measures to distance ourselves for the sake of the community, while our immediate neighbors treat this as no big deal. People bustle in-and-out of homes throughout this area and I well up with resent.

I've watched as our family of 5 have taken drastic measures to distance ourselves with a 14-year-old, a 12-year-old, and an adopted 3-year-old. They all have their own feelings and experiences with this. Last Friday was likely my 14-year-old's last day at the only school he's ever known. An unceremonious goodbye for the week was all that was given. Then, Friday night, the email came that the district was transitioning to online classes for both he and our 12-year-old daughter. Our 3-year-old son's early intervention schooling is no more. For a child who needs stability and closure, he's received none. For a child who's had (at least) two traumatic separations from important adults in his life - he now has another.

Both the oldest and the youngest have so many things they're giving up. The oldest is becoming quite the athlete. On a whim he decided to try out for the school's volleyball team. 53 boys tried out. 16 made the top team, and he was one of them. He's never played volleyball in his life. He's made one of the top-flight soccer teams in the area and was gearing up for his first season with them. He'd worked so hard to earn this spot, and then he kept working in order to ensure he wasn't just another kid on the team. Early February snows took away preseason tournaments, and now, in all likelihood, the season is cancelled. He's moving on to high school this upcoming Fall and had real expectations of making the JV team. But it was going to require work - work that he's not willing to put in because he feels robbed of the things that have brought him the most joy. I mourn the loss of his joy. I fear that he's making decisions that will set him back in his joy when this whole thing ends (in 18 months?).

My daughter is one of the fastest kids around. She's so fast that she almost made the top girls soccer team despite not having played soccer since she was 5, and that she's a sixth-grader in a school full of eight-grade talented athletes. She's a varsity XC runner and we were going to put her in a running team this spring to get her back into the love of it. Beyond this, though, she's a Renaissance girl. She's also dedicated this entire year to a choir elective where the end result would be a trip to Disneyland to sing on a stage in the park. This has been taken from her. She's a friend to all and loves spending time with others - and that has been taken away from her. The walks to meet friends at the local Starbucks are no more. They're gone.

In the grand scheme of things I know this is insignificant. People are dying. Peoples’ lives are truly being harmed. It's not a matter of, “oh no, my son won't make Varsity as a sophomore” or “my daughter can't make the trip to Disneyland” for so many people - people in my professional community. I sound like a whiner - and I am. We may never be able to fully quantify the fallout from what's happening in our world right now. There is a future in store for most of us, and I can't lose sight of that. But right now, it's hard.

Hope's a seed you have to sow.

When you let it go it comes to life

So you stretch your arrows on the bow

And you pull them back and watch them fly.

Right now I just have to hold onto hope for the future, while mourning the potential loss of what could have been. It's a dynamic tension that I'm not yet wise enough to know how to cut.

To all of my friends (whether you like me or not) who are out there dealing with the disastrous consequences of this pandemic - I'm sorry. You're dealing with so much more than I am. Forgive me for my selfishness.
